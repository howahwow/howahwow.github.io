Dom Sovetov

Completed

1987

Function

Government / Parliament

Location

Tiraspol, Transnistria

Style

Soviet Late Modernism

Photos

4

The Dom Sovetov — House of Soviets — is the seat of the Supreme Soviet of the Pridnestrovian Moldavian Republic, the unrecognised state that declared independence from Moldova in 1990 and has operated in a legal and political limbo ever since. The building itself was completed in 1987, four years before the Soviet Union collapsed, and it looks it: a late Soviet administrative block in the standard idiom of the era, its facade now painted in the red-green-red bands of the Transnistrian flag, with the word ДОМСОВЕТОВ mounted above the entrance alongside a functioning clock tower whose spire is topped with a Soviet star.

What makes this building unlike any other in Europe is the statue in front of it. Lenin stands here — arm outstretched, facing the square — as he does throughout the former Soviet Union. But in Tiraspol he has never been removed. Transnistria is the last territory in Europe where Lenin statues remain as official state monuments in their original positions, where Soviet-era symbols including the hammer and sickle appear on the state flag, and where the Soviet aesthetic of governance has been preserved not as nostalgia or irony but as ideology. To stand in this square is to stand in a place that has genuinely not moved on — and to understand why that claim is far more complicated than it first appears.
